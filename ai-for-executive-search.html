AI Intake for Executive Search Firms: Stop the PDF Problem

The Intake Problem No One Talks About in Retained Search

There is a quiet, expensive problem sitting at the front of every executive search pipeline: the intake form. Directors, VPs, and C-suite candidates are busy, selective, and acutely aware of how your process signals your positioning. When your firm sends a 20-field web form or a PDF intake packet to a sitting CFO at 11 p.m., you are not communicating that you are a retained search partner operating at her level. You are communicating that your back office was built for volume staffing, not for the relationship-first work that justifies your retainer fee.

The numbers make the damage concrete. Mobile form completion rates across the staffing industry hover around 20 percent. Four out of five candidates who click through to your intake form never finish it. In executive search, that drop-off is not a funnel metric — it is a candidate you lost to a competing firm that reached them faster and asked less of them in the first touchpoint.

The math compounds as intake requirements grow. Every additional field on a form reduces completion by roughly 10 percent. A thorough executive intake — covering compensation history and targets, relocation appetite, board or P&L scope, equity preferences, confidentiality considerations, and reference contacts — is mathematically destined to fail on a mobile device. You cannot build a 15-field intake and expect a VP-level candidate to finish it between meetings on an iPhone.

  • 20% mobile form completion rate — the industry baseline your competitors are also fighting
  • ~10% completion drop per additional field — every thorough executive intake is penalized by its own thoroughness
  • Lost relationship equity — candidates remember intake friction, not the opportunity your form was trying to unlock

The real cost is not missing data in your ATS. It is the relationship equity you forfeit in the first ten minutes. When a VP-level candidate abandons your intake form, they do not circle back. They move on — and they remember that your firm made them feel like an applicant, not a sought-after executive.
