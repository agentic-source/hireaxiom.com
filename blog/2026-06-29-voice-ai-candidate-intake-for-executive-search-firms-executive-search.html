The Intake Problem Executive Search Firms Don't Talk About

Executive search firms spend months cultivating relationships with senior candidates, then hand them a 15-field web form and hope for the best. It is a quiet contradiction nobody names out loud, and it is costing firms placements they should be closing.

The core dynamic: between 70 and 80 percent of executive-level candidates are passive. They are currently employed, not browsing job boards, and not inclined to stop what they are doing to fill out an intake packet. A VP of Operations or a General Counsel does not take a break from their day to type their compensation history into a form field. They answer the phone, or they do not engage at all.

The administrative overhead compounds the problem. A standard executive search runs 10 to 14 weeks in 2026, and the timeline is not driven by candidate scarcity. It is driven by scheduling friction, incomplete intake data, and the follow-up cycles that accumulate when forms go unfinished. Every day a recruiter spends chasing a half-completed intake packet is a day the search timeline slips.

The research is unambiguous on form completion: every additional field on a form reduces completion by roughly 10 percent. A 15-field executive intake form is not just inconvenient. It is mathematically engineered to lose candidates before they finish.

  • Brief drift — a rushed or incomplete initial intake — is the most common root cause of executive search failure. Intake quality is the highest-leverage step in the entire process.
  • Web forms, PDF packets, and SMS links send an unintentional message: that your retained search firm runs the same intake process as a warehouse staffing agency.
  • Mobile form completion rates in staffing hover around 20 percent. Four out of five candidates who click through never finish.

The firms winning passive senior candidates in 2026 are not the ones with the best forms. They are the ones who picked up the phone first.
